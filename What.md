# DEC and INC Signals: Algorithmic Trading Strategy

In this algorithmic trading strategy for electricity markets, **DEC** and **INC** signals represent specific types of bids based on the predicted relationship between **Day-Ahead (DA)** and **Real-Time (RT)** prices.

---

### **DEC Signal (Decrement Bid)**

- **Signal Value**: **+1**
- **Prediction**: The model forecasts that the **Real-Time (RT) price will be significantly higher than the Day-Ahead (DA) price** (RT > DA).
- **Action**: The trader **buys electricity in the Day-Ahead market** and **sells it in the Real-Time market**.
- **Profit Condition**: The strategy is profitable if the **Real-Time price is higher than the Day-Ahead price** at execution.
- **Interpretation**: This is analogous to taking a **"long" position** on the spread (RT - DA).

---

### **INC Signal (Increment Bid)**

- **Signal Value**: **-1**
- **Prediction**: The model forecasts that the **Day-Ahead (DA) price will be significantly higher than the Real-Time (RT) price** (DA > RT).
- **Action**: The trader **sells electricity in the Day-Ahead market** and **buys it back in the Real-Time market**.
- **Profit Condition**: The strategy is profitable if the **Day-Ahead price is higher than the Real-Time price** at execution.
- **Interpretation**: This is analogous to taking a **"short" position** on the spread (RT - DA).

---

### **Key Insights**

- **Spread Focus**: Both signals are designed to capitalize on the **price spread** between the Day-Ahead and Real-Time markets.
- **Conviction Threshold**: The model only generates signals when it predicts a spread exceeding a predefined threshold (e.g., $2.00/MWh), ensuring trades are based on high-conviction opportunities.
- **Risk Management**: By focusing on spreads, the strategy aims to hedge against absolute price movements and profit from relative price differences.

---

### **Visualization**

- **DEC Signal (+1)**: Green upward triangle (▲) on price charts, indicating a "long" spread position.
- **INC Signal (-1)**: Purple downward triangle (▼) on price charts, indicating a "short" spread position.

---

### **Why It Matters**

- **Market Efficiency**: These signals help traders exploit inefficiencies between Day-Ahead and Real-Time pricing.
- **Automation**: The model automates decision-making, reducing emotional bias and enabling rapid execution.
- **Scalability**: The strategy can be applied across different energy markets or timeframes with minimal adjustments.


# Backtest Performance Tear Sheet: Term Breakdown

Here’s a detailed explanation of each metric in your **BACKTEST PERFORMANCE TEAR SHEET**:

---

### **1. Total Simulation Time**

- **Definition**: The total duration, in hours, for which the trading strategy was simulated.
- **Example**: 2,880 hours = 120 days (2,880 ÷ 24).
- **Why It Matters**: Longer simulations provide more robust statistics but may also introduce overfitting risks if not handled carefully.

---

### **2. Trades Executed**

- **Definition**: The total number of individual buy or sell transactions (DEC or INC signals) executed by the algorithm during the simulation.
- **Example**: 2,465 trades.
- **Why It Matters**: High trade frequency can lead to higher transaction costs, impacting net profitability.

---

### **3. Hit Rate (Win %)**

- **Definition**: The percentage of trades that resulted in a **positive net profit** after accounting for fees.
- **Example**: 61.0% hit rate.
- **Why It Matters**: A higher hit rate indicates a more reliable strategy, but it must be balanced with the magnitude of wins vs. losses.

---

### **4. Gross PnL (Profit and Loss)**

- **Definition**: The **total profit or loss** generated by all trades **before deducting transaction fees**.
- **Example**: $X,XXX (e.g., $15,000).
- **Why It Matters**: Represents the raw performance of the strategy, but **does not account for real-world costs**.

---

### **5. Fees Paid**

- **Definition**: The **total amount spent on transaction fees** (e.g., clearing fees, exchange fees) across all executed trades.
- **Example**: $X,XXX (e.g., $2,500).
- **Why It Matters**: Fees directly reduce net profitability and are a critical factor in real-world trading.

---

### **6. Net PnL**

- **Definition**: The **ultimate profit or loss** of the strategy **after accounting for transaction fees** (Gross PnL - Fees Paid).
- **Example**: $X,XXX (e.g., $12,500).
- **Why It Matters**: This is your **bottom-line profit**—the most important metric for evaluating real-world viability.

---

### **7. Max Drawdown**

- **Definition**: The **largest peak-to-trough decline** in your portfolio’s cumulative equity (Net PnL) during the simulation.
- **Example**: -$841.26.
- **Why It Matters**: Measures the **worst-case loss** from a portfolio peak. High drawdowns can be psychologically and financially challenging.

---

### **8. Sharpe Ratio (Ann.)**

- **Definition**: A measure of **risk-adjusted return**, calculated as:  
`(Mean Return - Risk-Free Rate) / Standard Deviation of Returns`.
  - **"Ann."** stands for **annualized**, meaning the ratio is scaled to reflect yearly performance.
- **Example**: 21.60 (exceptionally high for simulated data).
- **Why It Matters**:
  - A **higher Sharpe Ratio** indicates better risk-adjusted performance.
  - In **real markets**, a Sharpe Ratio of **1-2 is good for equities**, while **3+ is excellent** (especially for niche or less liquid assets).
  - **Note**: Simulated data often inflates the Sharpe Ratio due to controlled volatility. Real-world results may vary significantly.

---

### **Key Takeaways**

- **Gross PnL** shows raw performance, but **Net PnL** is what matters in practice.
- **Hit Rate** and **Max Drawdown** help assess strategy reliability and risk.
- **Sharpe Ratio** is crucial for comparing strategies on a risk-adjusted basis.

# Max Drawdown: Explained Like You're 5

### **Imagine Your Trading Account is a Video Game Score**

- You start with **$0**.
- You trade well, and your money grows to **$1,000**. (Yay! High score!)
- Then, you lose some trades, and your money drops to **$800**. (Ouch!)
  - The drop from **$1,000 to $800** is a **$200 drawdown**.

---

### **Now, the Game Continues**

- You recover and your money grows to **$1,500**. (New high score!)
- But then, you hit a bad streak and your money falls to **$1,000**.
  - The drop from **$1,500 to $1,000** is a **$500 drawdown**.

---

### **What is Max Drawdown?**

- It’s the **biggest drop** your money ever took from its highest point to the lowest point after that.
- In this example, the **$500 drop** is the **Max Drawdown** because it’s the worst loss you experienced from a peak.

---

### **Why Does It Matter?**

- It tells you: **"This is the most money I could have lost from my highest point."**
- If your Max Drawdown is **$841.26**, it means at some point, your account lost **$841.26** from its highest value before it started recovering (or dropped even more).
- It’s like the game telling you: **"The worst you ever did was lose $841.26 from your best score."**

---

### **Real-Life Example**

- Suppose your trading account grows to **$10,000** (peak!).
- Then, it drops to **$9,158.74** before recovering.
- The **Max Drawdown** is **$841.26** ($10,000 - $9,158.74).

---

### **Why Should You Care?**

- It helps you understand **how much pain your strategy can cause** before it (hopefully) bounces back.
- A smaller Max Drawdown means your strategy is **less risky** and easier to stick with.
- A bigger Max Drawdown means you need to be **prepared for big drops** and have the patience to wait for recovery.

---

### **Summary**

Max Drawdown = **"The worst single drop in my money from its highest point."**

It’s like the game’s "lowest moment" after you hit your best score. In trading, it’s the **biggest loss** you’d have to stomach if you held on during the worst stretch.
